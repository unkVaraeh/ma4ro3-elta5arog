Welcome to Luxor

Luxor is a historic city in southern Egypt, often referred to as the "world's greatest open-air museum." It is home to famous ancient monuments, including the Valley of the Kings, Karnak Temple, and Luxor Temple. The city was once the capital of ancient Egypt during the New Kingdom and continues to attract visitors with its rich archaeological and cultural heritage.

Where to go in Luxor

Valley of the Kings

The Valley of the Kings is a famous archaeological site in Luxor, Egypt, where tombs of ancient Egyptian pharaohs and nobles are located. It served as the burial ground for the New Kingdom rulers, including Tutankhamun and Ramses II. The valley is renowned for its richly decorated tombs and elaborate wall paintings, offering valuable insights into ancient Egyptian burial practices and beliefs.

Karnak Temple

The Karnak Temple complex is one of the largest and most impressive ancient religious sites in Egypt, located in Luxor. It was primarily dedicated to the Theban triad of Amun, Mut, and Khonsu, and construction spanned over 2,000 years, with contributions from numerous pharaohs. The temple is famous for its grand Hypostyle Hall, towering obelisks, and massive pylons, showcasing the architectural and artistic achievements of ancient Egypt.

Der El-Madina

Deir el-Medina is an ancient village located on the west bank of the Nile near Luxor, known for being the home of the workers who built the tombs in the Valley of the Kings. The village is well-preserved, with houses, workshops, and a small temple, offering valuable insight into the daily life of these craftsmen and their families. The site is also famous for its detailed wall paintings and inscriptions, providing a glimpse into the social, religious, and cultural practices of ancient Egypt.

Temple Of Hatshepsut

The Temple of Hatshepsut is an ancient Egyptian temple located in the Valley of the Kings, near Luxor. Built by Queen Hatshepsut during the 18th Dynasty, it is dedicated to the god Amun and to her own reign as pharaoh. The temple is renowned for its unique architectural design, with terraced levels and colonnades that blend harmoniously with the surrounding cliffs, making it one of Egypt's most striking monuments.

Colossi Of Memnon

The Colossi of Memnon are two massive statues of Pharaoh Amenhotep III, located on the west bank of the Nile near Luxor. These towering statues, each standing about 18 meters tall, once flanked the entrance to Amenhotep's mortuary temple, which is now in ruins. Known for their impressive size and mysterious "singing" sound at dawn, the Colossi are a popular tourist attraction and a symbol of ancient Egyptian grandeur.

The Tomp Of Queen Nefertari

The Tomb of Queen Nefertari is located in the Valley of the Queens in Luxor, Egypt, and is considered one of the most beautiful and well-preserved tombs from ancient Egypt. It is the final resting place of Queen Nefertari, the beloved wife of Pharaoh Ramses II. The tomb is famous for its vibrant and intricate wall paintings, depicting scenes of the queen in the afterlife, showcasing her importance and the high regard in which she was held.

Luxor Tempale

The Luxor Temple is an ancient Egyptian temple located in the heart of Luxor, dedicated to the god Amun, his wife Mut, and their son Khonsu. It was constructed during the New Kingdom, primarily under Pharaoh Amenhotep III and later expanded by Ramses II. The temple is renowned for its stunning architecture, massive statues, and the Avenue of Sphinxes, which connects it to Karnak Temple.
